Strategy

Play smart: big margins, simple choices, relentless consistency — then strike when you’ve earned it.

Strategy

Strategy is your match-wide plan. It decides how you want points to feel before you worry about individual patterns.

It should stay simple enough that you can repeat it out loud on a changeover and remember it when you’re tired.

Coach’s Cue: “Plan → Breathe → Commit.” If you don’t have a plan, default to a high, deep ball to the middle and reset.

Most of the strategy on this page applies to both singles and doubles. When something matters more for one format, you’ll see a small tag like [Singles Focus] or [Doubles Focus] so you can connect it directly to how you play for Poolesville.

Core Pillars

These are your non-negotiables in both singles and doubles. If a point goes badly, check which pillar you ignored.

  • Percentages: Aim for big targets, high net clearance, and spin. If you miss, miss long — not in the tape.
  • Pressure: Build height and depth first; look to finish after you’ve moved the opponent out of position.
  • Patience: Stay in your patterns. Win “boring” points by making them hit one more quality ball.
  • Position: Recover inside the hash. Own the middle until you’ve clearly earned the angle.

Standard: In a match, 65–70% of points should start with a high-margin target (deep middle or body).

Between-Point Routine (BPR)

Use a short, repeatable routine to connect your strategy to every single point — singles or doubles. Same routine, different targets.

  1. Plan: Name your target and purpose out loud (build / pressure / finish). [Singles Focus] Say “deep cross then middle” or “heavy to the backhand.” [Doubles Focus] Say “middle on return” or “serve wide, partner pinches.”
  2. Breathe: 4-count inhale, 6-count exhale; relax jaw, hands, and shoulders.
  3. Commit: Use a one-word cue at bounce (“Height” / “Middle” / “Up”) and trust it.

If you feel doubt or panic, go back to “deep middle with height” and rebuild from there.

Scoreboard Rules

The score should change your margin, not your identity. Use these simple defaults in both singles and doubles.

  • Up 30–0 / 40–15: Add pressure with depth; look for angles on the second ball, not the first. [Doubles Focus] With the lead, challenge the middle more and let the net player hunt.
  • 30–30 / Deuce: Highest-margin targets only — deep middle or body. [Singles Focus] Force them to play one more solid rally ball. [Doubles Focus] Hit middle and make them decide “whose ball is this?”
  • Break Points (for or against): Serve or return to the big target you trust most and play first ball with margin.
  • Tie-breakers: Prioritize first-ball height; no off-balance “hero” swings.

Target: Hold serve 75%+ by playing first-ball to margin.

Environment & Adjustments

Conditions don’t change who you are as a player, but they do change the safest way to express your game. Great players adjust to the environment faster than their opponents.

Wind

On windy days, your job is to simplify — bigger margins, higher net clearance, cleaner targets.

  • [Singles Focus] Play more heavy cross-court, especially into the wind. Avoid low, flat shots the wind can shove long or wide.
  • [Doubles Focus] Use the wind to your advantage by aiming through the middle first. Make the other team guess who should take it.
  • Into the wind – Think “drive and brave.” Hit a bit more through the ball with solid racquet speed, aim a little deeper, and still use spin. The wind is your brake.
  • With the wind – Think “high and heavy, land it shorter.” Add topspin, not just height, and aim 2–3 feet shorter than your normal deep target. Let the wind carry the ball.

Sun

When the sun is in your eyes, you need a clear plan for toss height, overheads, and lobs.

  • [Singles Focus] If one side has brutal glare, use that side to break serve and protect your own service games on the easier side.
  • [Doubles Focus] Decide before the point who will take balls that go into the sun, especially lobs in the middle, and say it out loud.
  • Favor cross-court because the net is lower and the court is longer, and use higher net clearance so you’re not aiming at tiny, hard-to-see targets. If vision is really compromised, send a calm cross-court lob to buy time and reset your eyes.

Surface & Bounce

Hard courts, slower courts, and bouncy courts all reward slightly different decisions.

  • [Singles Focus] On slower or higher-bouncing courts, lean into heavy topspin and patience. Make your opponent hit one more ball.
  • [Doubles Focus] On faster courts, commit to first-strike doubles: aggressive returns, strong first volleys, and decisive poaches.
  • After warmup, say out loud what the bounce is doing (“skidding low” vs “jumping up”) and choose Serve+1, Return+1, and net patterns that fit that bounce.

Standard: After a side change, play the next game with zero tape errors by using your wind, sun, and surface adjustments on every first ball.

Strategic Game Plans

These are simple “big-picture” plans. Tactics on the next page are how you execute them point by point. Each plan can be used in singles and doubles with small tweaks.

Serve-First Plan

Serve to big targets, play the first ball deep middle, then finish into open space.

  • [Singles Focus] Serve wide → hit deep cross → finish to the open court.
  • [Doubles Focus] Serve wide → partner pinches middle looking to poach.

Goal: Win 65–70% of Serve+1 exchanges.

See Tactics for Serve+1 trees.

Return-First Plan

Neutralize with height to the body or deep middle; step in on second serves.

  • [Singles Focus] Heavy cross-court return, then control middle with depth.
  • [Doubles Focus] Return middle to shrink angles and let your partner attack.

Goal: Win about 45% of return games by forcing a short ball in the first three shots.

See Tactics for Return+1 branches.

Neutral to Offense

Rally cross-court until you see short / middle / height, then take space and pressure.

[Singles Focus] Look to step inside the baseline on anything short in the middle. [Doubles Focus] When you pull them wide, your partner looks to finish at the net.

Cue: “Short or middle? Go.”

Defense to Reset

Raise height, aim deep middle, and recover inside the hash to buy time.

[Singles Focus] High, heavy to deep middle, recover and restart. [Doubles Focus] Reset high and deep through the middle so your team can regroup.

Goal: Reset to neutral within two balls at least 6/10 times.

Opponent Archetypes

Use simple labels so your team can agree quickly on what you’re facing, then choose a plan. These show up in both singles and doubles; the patterns just look a little different.

  • Big Hitter: Add height and depth; make them hit one more ball from uncomfortable positions. [Doubles Focus] Make them hit from low-and-wide positions, then let your net player finish.
  • Counter-puncher: Take balls early inside the court; finish to space, not to the line.
  • Net Rusher: Target body and feet first; lob or pass only when balanced. [Doubles Focus] Hit low through the middle to jam both players before you try the big angle.
  • All-Court: Win the middle and vary height and tempo to force them into making decisions.

Singles vs Doubles: Quick Strategy Snapshot

Singles Strategy Snapshot

  • Make the court feel big for them, small for you with deep, safe targets.
  • Have a clear “home base” pattern you trust at big moments.
  • Be patient: let your opponent miss first instead of forcing low-percentage winners.

Doubles Strategy Snapshot

  • Win with position + communication as much as shot-making.
  • Use the net player as the boss of the point — active, threatening, ready to poach.
  • Default to middle + cross: middle returns, middle volleys, and smart crosses at the right time.

Before each match, choose one singles goal and one doubles goal from this list to focus on. That’s how you build strategy that actually shows up when you compete.

Match Metrics & Review

Use simple numbers to check whether your strategy actually showed up in the match. Some will skew more singles, some more doubles — but all keep you honest.

  • First-Ball Margin: 80%+ of first balls clear the net by 2–3 feet.
  • Serve+1 Win%: 65–70% target in practice sets.
  • Neutral Rally Wins: About 60% in rallies under 6 balls; 55% in rallies over 6 balls.
  • Emotional Errors: At most 1 rushed, low-margin swing per set.
  • [Doubles] Poach Attempts: Aim for at least 3–5 real poach attempts per set.

Post-Match: Write down one principle you kept well and one you’ll emphasize next match.

Strategy Checklist

Use this as a quick reset between sets or before practice. Run it for both singles and doubles.

  • ⬜ First ball to a big target with height
  • ⬜ Recover inside the hash after every swing
  • ⬜ Decide the purpose before serve/return (build / pressure / finish)
  • ⬜ Run your BPR every point: Plan → Breathe → Commit
  • ⬜ Name one [Singles Focus] and one [Doubles Focus] goal before the match

Weekly Challenge: In two practice sets, keep a simple BPR note on your towel and track Serve+1 win%, first-ball margin, and (for doubles) real poach attempts.

Coach’s Corner

Strategy

Focus Theme

Strategy is your program compass. Think in three layers — Principles → Plans → Plays — so every coach and player is speaking the same language on match day in both singles and doubles.

Layer 1 — Principles (Non-Negotiables)

  • Win with margin: height, depth, and spin first; “miss long, never in the tape.”
  • Neutral → Advantage → Finish: build pressure before going for the hero ball.
  • Own the middle: recover inside the hash until you’ve earned the angle.
  • One plan per point: choose build / pressure / finish in the BPR and stick to it.
  • Cool head, clear cue: players respond to simple cues (“Height”, “Middle”, “Up”) not speeches.

Layer 2 — Plans (Match & Set Plans)

  • Serve-First Plan: big targets on serve, first ball deep middle, finish to space. Pair this with Scoreboard Rules at 30–30, deuce, and break points. Add a version for singles and a version for doubles lineups.
  • Return-First Plan: neutralize to body or deep middle, step in on seconds, live cross-court until you earn short.
  • Neutral-to-Offense Plan: stay cross-court until you see short / middle / height, then attack.
  • Defense-to-Reset Plan: raise height, aim deep middle, recover inside the hash within two balls.
  • For each player, pick one primary plan to start the match and one backup to try after a side change.

Layer 3 — Plays (Point-Level Patterns)

  • “Two cross, one change”: build with two cross-court balls, change direction only when balanced.
  • “Deep middle reset”: under pressure, send a high, heavy ball deep middle and recover.
  • “Short or middle? Go.”: if they leave the ball short or middle, step in and pressure space.
  • “High then low”: lift deep with height, then pull them short (drop or short angle).
  • Use Tactics as the detailed playbook: Serve+1 trees, Return+1 branches, and pattern cards for both singles and doubles.

Coach Implementation Drill

In a team meeting, pick one upcoming match. As a staff (and captains), define: 2 core principles to emphasize, 1 primary plan per player (Serve-First, Return-First, or Neutral-to-Offense), and 2 go-to plays from the Tactics page. Label each as primarily [Singles], [Doubles], or Both. Write them on a simple card or in a notes app. Goal: after the match, each player can explain their plan and name at least one play they actually used under pressure.
