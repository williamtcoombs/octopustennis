Tactics

Strategy is your map for the match. Tactics are the point-by-point play calls that make that map real. Every pattern on this page is a concrete way to express the Four P’s (Percentages, Pressure, Patience, Position) from your Strategy page.

Before each point, use your Between-Point Routine (BPR) to choose one pattern that fits the situation: serve or return, singles or doubles, neutral or under pressure. Say it quietly to yourself (or to your partner), then run it.

Think of this page as your Pattern Menu: Serve+1 trees, Return+1 schemes, neutral rally patterns, transition and net plays, and doubles systems that line up directly with your Strategic Game Plans (Serve-First, Return-First, Neutral-to-Offense, Defense-to-Reset).

Coach’s Cue: “Plan → Pattern → Point.” Use the Strategy page to choose the plan, this Tactics page to choose the pattern, and your BPR to stay present for the next point.

Top Strategy Principles in Action

On the Strategy page you saw the “Top 10 Strategy Principles.” This section turns each one into a default tactical behavior you can actually run: clear targets, simple cues, and links to patterns on this page.

Use it like a checklist: if a point or game goes sideways, find the principle you broke, then pick the matching play and run it for the next game.

  1. Own the Middle → “Recover Inside the Hash.”

    After every shot, recover so you can touch the center hash with one or two steps. In rallies, aim deep middle until you’ve clearly earned width.

    Pair with: Middle Win.

  2. Play High-Percentage Targets → “Big Zones, Big Net Clearance.”

    Choose cross-court or deep middle 80–90% of the time on neutral balls. Add 2–3 feet of net clearance as your default, especially when nervous.

    Pair with: Height First.

  3. Move Them Before You Finish → “Two to Move, One to Hurt.”

    Commit to two rally balls that push them deep or wide before you go for the finish. Don’t pull the trigger until you’ve created real space.

    Pair with: cross-court rallies from Live Cross, then transition patterns in Transition & Net.

  4. Balance Before Power → “No Hero Swings Off-Balance.”

    If you’re drifting, falling, or stretched, your only job is to reset: high and deep, usually through the middle. Save aggressive swings for when both feet are stable.

    Pair with: Special Situations “Big Points” plus Height First.

  5. Use Depth to Stabilize → “Deep Middle Reset.”

    Any time the rally feels chaotic, send a high, heavy ball deep middle, then recover inside the hash. Make them play from the back of the court again.

    Pair with: Depth Lock.

  6. Know Your Patterns → “Name Your A-Play.”

    Have one “go-to” pattern on serve and one on return. Say it before big points: “Wide serve, +1 deep middle” or “Cross-court return, recover middle.”

    Pair with: Serve+1 Trees and Return+1 Schemes.

  7. Target Their Weakness → “Lean Without Becoming Predictable.”

    Aim most neutral balls at their weaker side or weaker movement direction, but occasionally change direction or height so they can’t sit on one look.

    Pair with: archetype plans via Pressure Packages and Doubles Systems.

  8. Recover to the Right Spot → “Center Between Threats.”

    After you hit, recover to the point that’s halfway between where their best two replies could land. Usually that’s just inside the singles court, slightly toward where you just hit.

    Pair with: recovery language in Neutral Patterns.

  9. Play the Score → “Risk Matches the Moment.”

    On big points (30–30, deuce, tie-break), default to your highest-margin patterns: deep middle or body, then build pressure. Use your creative stuff when you’re ahead in the game.

    Pair with: Serve+1, Return+1, and Pressure Packages.

  10. Make One More Ball → “Boring Wins Under Pressure.”

    When nerves spike, your only job is to put one more heavy, high-margin ball in play. No line hunting, no last-second changes — just depth, shape, and recovery.

    Pair with: “boring” patterns from Neutral Patterns and resets from Special Situations.

Coach’s Cue: When a point goes wrong, don’t guess. Ask “Which principle did we break?” and then choose the matching play from this list for the next game.

Core Pillars in Motion: Four P’s & Four T’s

On the Strategy page, the Four P’s (Percentages, Pressure, Patience, Position) and the Four T’s (Turn, T, Talk, Touch) define how we want Poolesville to play. This section turns them into default tactical behaviors you can choose before each point.

When a match feels messy, don’t invent something new. Pick one P and, in doubles, commit to the Four T’s between every point. Then choose a matching pattern from this page and run it for the next game.

Percentages → “Big Targets, Big Shape”

Percentages means we win with big zones, high net clearance, and safe directions. Your tactical job: choose the play that gives you the largest margin, especially under pressure.

  • Singles: Default to cross-court or deep middle with 2–3 feet of net clearance on neutral balls.
  • Doubles: Default to deep middle or body on serve and return to shrink angles.
  • Pattern Pair: Use Neutral Patterns (“Live Cross,” “Middle Win,” “Height First”) plus the Tactical Checklist to keep your first ball high-percentage.

Pressure → “Stack Good Balls Before You Finish”

Pressure is not “swing harder.” It’s stacking quality balls that push the opponent into weaker positions until they crack or give you an easy finish.

  • Singles: Commit to two or three deep, heavy balls before looking for a winner. Think “move first, finish second.”
  • Doubles: Use body serves, deep middle returns, and active net movement to squeeze space and force bad contact.
  • Pattern Pair: Call a Pressure Package (“Body First,” “Depth Lock,” “Height Lock”) when you’re up 30–0 / 40–15 to raise pressure without getting reckless.

Patience → “Win the Boring Point”

Patience means you’re willing to play the longer, smarter rally instead of chasing a low-percentage finish. You trust that consistent, heavy balls will draw errors.

  • Singles: On tough days, commit to “no hero down-the-line” unless you’re inside the court and balanced.
  • Doubles: Keep the ball cross-court and middle until you’ve clearly earned a short ball or high volley. Let opponents get impatient first.
  • Pattern Pair: Use Neutral Patterns and the “boring wins” mindset from Special Situations whenever you feel rushed or frustrated.

Position → “Own the Middle, Then Earn the Angle”

Position is about where you stand and where you recover. Our default: own the middle until you’ve truly earned space.

  • Singles: Recover just inside the singles court and slightly toward where you hit. Don’t camp in the alley unless the point clearly demands it.
  • Doubles: Move as a unit — when one player shifts, the other mirrors to keep the middle covered.
  • Pattern Pair: Combine recovery cues from Neutral Patterns with forward movement in Transition & Net (approach → first volley → close).

[Doubles Focus] Four T’s → “Reset After Every Point”

The Four T’s keep your connection and communication strong no matter the score. Treat them as a tactical rule between every doubles point.

  • Turn: First step goes toward your partner, not the fence or your bag.
  • T: Meet at, or glance toward, the “T” (center/serve line cross) to reset as a unit.
  • Talk: One clear phrase: recognition (“Nice depth”), encouragement (“We’re fine, big margins here”), or plan (“Middle return, I’ll poach”).
  • Touch: Racquets, fist bump, or shoulder tap — a tiny action that says, “We’re in this together.”

Doubles Standard: In every set, aim for Four T’s on at least 90% of points. If communication drops, captains call a quick reset.

Coach’s Cue: “Pick one P and play to it.” If you feel lost tactically, choose Percentages, Pressure, Patience, or Position as your focus for the next two games and select patterns from this page that match it.

BPR in Action: Call-the-Play Scripts

On the Strategy page, your Between-Point Routine (BPR) gives you the steps: Plan → Breathe → Commit. This section turns that routine into short, repeatable scripts you can use to call patterns from this Tactics page.

Use these like fill-in-the-blank huddles. Before each point, plug in: the score, the situation (serve / return, singles / doubles), and one pattern from this page that fits your strategy focus.

Step 1 — Plan: “What’s the play?”

In the first few seconds after a point ends, quietly answer three questions:

  • Scoreboard: “Am I ahead, behind, or even in this game?”
  • Strengths: “Which pattern fits my strengths here?”
  • Target: “Where is the first ball going?”

Then choose one pattern from: Serve+1 Trees, Return+1 Schemes, or Neutral Patterns.

Step 2 — Breathe: “Slow the movie down.”

Before you walk up to the line or return position, take one full breath: in through your nose, out through your mouth. Use that exhale to let go of the last point.

  • Physical cue: Drop your shoulders, soften your jaw, release your grip 10–20%.
  • Mental cue: Repeat a short phrase: “Next point only” or “Big margins here.”
  • Pattern cue: Picture the first ball of your chosen pattern: its height, shape, and target.

Step 3 — Commit: “Say it and own it.”

Commitment means you stop shopping for other ideas once you’ve picked the play. Say a short script to yourself (or your partner), then trust it fully for that point.

Use these sample scripts as a starting point:

  • [Singles Serve] “Score is 30–15. Plan: Body serve, +1 deep middle. Commit: ‘Body, middle, recover.’”
  • [Singles Return] “Score is 15–30. Plan: Cross-court return, recover middle. Commit: ‘Big margin cross, middle first.’”
  • [Doubles Serve Team] “Score is deuce. Plan: T serve, partner poach on the next ball. Commit: ‘T serve, middle first volley.’”
  • [Doubles Return Team] “Score is 30–30. Plan: Deep middle return, net player active. Commit: ‘Middle return, you ready to move.’”

BPR Pattern Pairings

To make BPR automatic, pre-choose a few pairings you’ll lean on this season:

  • Serve Game Default: BPR + one Serve+1 Tree. Example: “Wide serve, +1 deep middle” for two whole games.
  • Return Game Default: BPR + one Return+1 Scheme. Example: “Cross-court return, recover middle” for a full return game.
  • Neutral Rally Default: BPR + one Neutral Pattern. Example: “Live Cross” with “Height First” on tough balls.
  • Pressure Default: BPR + one Pressure Package when the score gets tight.

Match Standard: In every set, aim to run your BPR on at least 80% of points. Captains: if you see rushing, call out, “BPR first, then play.”

Coach’s Cue: “Don’t just reset your mind — reset your pattern.” Use BPR not just to feel calmer, but to choose one clear play from this page before every serve or return.

Scoreboard Rules in Action: Green, Yellow, Red

On the Strategy page, the Scoreboard Rules tell you how to adjust risk by score instead of emotion. This section turns that idea into simple packages you can call before each point: Green (go), Yellow (steady), Red (lock it down).

Use these like traffic lights for your tactics. Check the score, pick the color, then choose one pattern from this page that fits that level of risk.

Green Light Points → “Earned Risk”

Green Light points are when the score gives you cushion: 30–0, 40–15, 40–0 on your serve; 0–30 or 15–40 on theirs. This is when you can test a new pattern or be a little more aggressive — but still inside your identity.

  • Singles: Try a trusted direction change (cross → line) or a planned short angle after you’ve moved them.
  • Doubles: Call a planned poach or a more aggressive return that targets the net player’s feet.
  • Pattern Pair: Combine Green Light points with Transition & Net and your favorite Serve+1 Tree.

Yellow Light Points → “Big Margins, Clear Patterns”

Yellow Light points are neutral or early in the game: 15–15, 30–30, 0–0, 15–0, 0–15. Here you stick to your A-plan patterns: big targets, high margins, no surprise changes unless you’ve clearly earned them.

  • Singles: Default to cross-court or deep middle with 2–3 feet of net clearance; play “Two to move, one to hurt.”
  • Doubles: Serve and return body or middle, then play through the middle until a clear short ball appears.
  • Pattern Pair: Use Yellow Light points to run Neutral Patterns and your standard Return+1 Scheme.

Red Light Points → “Lock the Doors”

Red Light points are the pressure moments: 30–40, break point, deuce, tie-break points, or when you’re serving to stay in the set. Here, we don’t get creative — we get disciplined.

  • Singles: First ball goes deep middle or safe cross-court. No hunting lines or last-second changes of mind.
  • Doubles: Serve body, return body or middle, and make the net player earn any poach with a strong read — no free gifts.
  • Pattern Pair: Attach Red Light points to your highest-confidence Serve+1, Return+1, and one Pressure Package (“Depth Lock” or “Body First”).

[Doubles Focus] Game-Score Packages

In doubles, let the game score decide how bold your pattern will be. Captains and partners can use these short calls between points:

  • Up 30–0 / 40–15: “Green — poach on anything short.” Pair with an aggressive Doubles System (Australian / I-Formation).
  • Even 15–15 / 30–30: “Yellow — body first, middle second.” Use safe serves/returns and trust Neutral Patterns.
  • Down 30–40 / Break Point: “Red — deep middle, no hero ball.” Run a pressure-safe pattern from Pressure Packages.

Scoreboard Scripts + BPR

Tie your Scoreboard Rules directly into your BPR in Action routine:

  • Green Script: “Up 40–15 — Green. Test the wide serve pattern.”
  • Yellow Script: “30–30 — Yellow. Big margin cross, recover middle.”
  • Red Script: “Break point — Red. Body serve, +1 deep middle. No line hunting.”

Match Standard: On every big point, you should be able to say your color (Green / Yellow / Red) and the pattern you’re running before the serve.

Coach’s Cue: “Let the score choose the risk.” When in doubt, drop back to Yellow or Red rules and run your safest patterns from this page until the match settles down.

Environment & Adjustments in Action

On the Strategy page, you named how wind, sun, and surface change the way a match feels. This section turns those notes into default play calls you can use so the environment helps you instead of rattling you.

Use a simple rule at changeovers: “Name the factor → Name the adjustment → Name the pattern.” Then pick one play from Neutral Patterns or Special Situations and run it for the next game.

Wind Plans → “High, Deep, and Smart Targets”

Wind can make good players look wild. Your job is not to fight it — it’s to build a pattern that works with it.

  • Into the wind: Add height and spin, aim deep middle, and expect one more ball to come back. Let the wind slow the ball down for you.
  • With the wind: Aim lower over the net and more toward middle so the ball doesn’t sail. Think “shape first, pace second.”
  • Cross-wind: Rally mostly cross-court into the wind so misses land long on the “safe” side, not wide.
  • Pattern Pair: Combine wind adjustments with Height First and the windy-day defaults from Special Situations.

Sun & Visibility → “Protect the Serve, Protect the Overhead”

Sun and glare mostly affect serves, overheads, and high balls. Your adjustments should protect those shots and steer play into patterns you can see clearly.

  • Serving into the sun: Use more body and T serves with shape instead of flat wide bombs. Make them look into the sun, not you.
  • Overheads in the sun: Call “lob-light” for a game: fewer offensive lobs, more deep cross-court resets that keep the ball below eye level.
  • Doubles tweak: When your partner is looking into the sun at net, call patterns that keep balls lower through the middle instead of hanging lobs.
  • Pattern Pair: Attach sun plans to Serve+1 Trees (body/T first) and the “Wind / Sun” defaults in Special Situations.

Surface & Bounce → “Match Your Shape to the Court”

Different courts reward different shapes of ball. Instead of complaining about the surface, choose patterns that the court rewards.

  • Faster / lower-bouncing courts: Aim deeper and more through the middle, use Serve+1 and Return+1 patterns to take time away early.
  • Slower / higher-bouncing courts: Use heavy spin and height, trust longer rallies, and lean into Neutral Patterns and Depth Lock.
  • Uneven bounces: Default to “high, heavy middle” and move your feet early. Let the bad bounce beat the ball, not your body.
  • Pattern Pair: On tricky surfaces, tie your plan to Transition & Net: approach on your best-bouncing ball, then close and finish at the net.

Environment Scripts + BPR

Tie Environment & Adjustments directly into your BPR so players name the factor and the fix before each game:

  • Wind Script: “Strong cross-wind — Height First, play high deep middle.”
  • Sun Script: “Serving into sun — body serves only, +1 to deep middle.”
  • Surface Script: “Fast court — Serve+1 to the body, look for first strike.”
  • [Doubles Focus] Team Script: “Wind off the right — we play middle-heavy, poach only on short balls.”

Match Standard: At each changeover, players can name one environment factor, one adjustment, and one pattern they’re using because of it.

Coach’s Cue: “The conditions aren’t the excuse; they’re the game plan.” Use this section with Special Situations so windy, sunny, or weird-bounce days become automatic pattern days, not chaos days.

Strategic Game Plans in Action

On the Strategy page, you saw four big Game Plans: Serve-First, Return-First, Neutral-to-Offense, and Defense-to-Reset. This section turns each one into a pattern tree you can actually call for a game.

Use them like scripts: at a changeover, pick one Game Plan and say, “For this game we are in <Plan> mode.” Then choose a matching pattern from the sections below (Serve+1, Return+1, Neutral, Transition & Net, and Pressure Packages).

Serve-First Plan → “Win the First Strike”

You call this when you trust your serve and first ball. The goal is to hold serve efficiently and apply early pressure.

  • [Singles Focus] Script: “Body or wide serve, +1 deep middle, then attack open space only when balanced.”
  • [Doubles Focus] Script: “Body serve, net player active. +1 deep middle; look for poach on the second ball.”
  • Pattern Tree: Serve+1 Trees + scoreboard-aware choices from Pressure Packages (for example, “Body First,” “Depth Lock” on big points).
  • Coach Cue: “This game is about first strike, not hero winners.”

Return-First Plan → “Neutralize, Then Squeeze”

You call this when the opponent’s serve is strong or streaky. The goal is to get the serve back with margin and force extra balls.

  • [Singles Focus] Script: “Big-margin cross-court return, recover middle, then play high, heavy neutral until they leak.”
  • [Doubles Focus] Script: “Deep middle or body return, net player ready but patient. No line hunting on first return.”
  • Pattern Tree: Return+1 Schemes + stabilizing plays from Neutral Patterns (“Live Cross,” “Middle Win”).
  • Coach Cue: “Job one: make the return. Job two: own the middle.”

Neutral-to-Offense Plan → “Two to Move, One to Hurt”

You call this when rallies are even and you need a clear way to turn neutral balls into pressure without rushing.

  • [Singles Focus] Script: “Two heavy cross-courts to move them, then attack the short ball to open space.”
  • [Doubles Focus] Script: “Cross-court and middle first; net player looks to close only once we’ve pushed them back.”
  • Pattern Tree: Rally shapes from Neutral Patterns → then forward movement through Transition & Net (approach → first volley → close).
  • Coach Cue: “Win the boring ball first, then earn the big one.”

Defense-to-Reset Plan → “Survive, Reset, Rebuild”

You call this when you’re under pressure: they’re dictating, you’re stretched, or the scoreboard is tense. The goal is to reset the point, not flip it in one swing.

  • [Singles Focus] Script: “Any time I’m stretched, high heavy ball deep middle, recover inside the hash, and rebuild from there.”
  • [Doubles Focus] Script: “Lob-light mode: high, deep middle resets or safe lobs when attacked; both players recover to protect middle.”
  • Pattern Tree: Reset plays from Special Situations (“Wind / Sun,” “Big Points”) + safety-first options from Depth Lock and Height First.
  • Coach Cue: “First we survive the attack, then we earn our turn.”

Match Standard: At every changeover, players can name which Game Plan they’re in and one pattern they’re using to express it.

Coach’s Cue: “Plan the game, then run the tree.” Choose Serve-First, Return-First, Neutral-to-Offense, or Defense-to-Reset, then live in the matching pattern family for a full game instead of improvising every point.

Opponent Archetypes & Formations in Action

On the Strategy page, you named opponent types and doubles formations so matches feel less random. This section turns those labels into counter-patterns you can call on the fly.

Use a simple sequence: Name → Aim → Pattern. Name the archetype or formation, choose a primary aim (middle, body, height, or width), then pick a pattern from this page that stresses what they do worst.

[Singles Focus] Common Archetypes → Counter-Patterns

These are default responses. You can always layer in your own strengths, but start here.

  • Big Hitter → “Shrink the Court, Raise the Net.”

    Goal: take away their targets and force them to hit over a higher “virtual net.” Live in deep middle, high margin, and make them earn every angle.

    • Aim: Deep middle and body, especially on big points.
    • Pattern Pair: Neutral Patterns (“Middle Win,” “Height First”) + Pressure Packages (“Depth Lock”).
    • Coach Cue: “Make them beat you through the middle, not with highlight shots.”
  • Counter-Puncher / Defender → “Own the Middle, Finish at the Net.”

    Goal: avoid endless rallies. Use depth to push them back, then finish forward instead of trying to blast through them from the baseline.

    • Aim: Deep cross-court and middle until you earn a short ball, then approach.
    • Pattern Pair: Cross-courts from Live Cross → then forward movement via Transition & Net.
    • Coach Cue: “Don’t try to out-grind the grinder — take the net.”
  • Pusher / Human Backboard → “Boring Wins with Planned Finishes.”

    Goal: stay patient but not passive. You win by repeating heavy, safe balls and having a planned finish (short angle, approach, or net).

    • Aim: High, heavy cross-courts that move them, then finish to open space or at net.
    • Pattern Pair: “Win the boring ball” from Neutral Patterns + a chosen finish from Transition & Net.
    • Coach Cue: “Heavy, patient, then one committed finish — not ten half-finished ideas.”
  • All-Courter / Problem Solver → “Anchor to Your Identity.”

    Goal: avoid getting dragged into their variety show. Keep coming back to your A-patterns and make them prove they can beat that identity.

    • Aim: Play into the patterns you trained most (forehand-favored, net-first, etc.).
    • Pattern Pair: Use your chosen Serve+1 and Return+1 “A-plays,” plus your favorite Neutral Pattern.
    • Coach Cue: “Win with your game first; adjust only if they truly solve it.”

[Doubles Focus] Common Formations → Team Responses

In doubles, the formation tells you where the space is. Use it to pick simple team patterns instead of guessing.

  • Both Back → “Take the Net and Own the Middle.”

    Goal: punish passive court position. Close the net together and force them to come up with low-percentage passes.

    • Aim: Approach to deep middle, first volley deep middle, then close.
    • Pattern Pair: Transition & Net (“Approach → First Volley → Close”) + middle-first language from Doubles Systems.
    • Team Cue: “Both up, protect middle, live with the occasional pass.”
  • One Up / One Back → “Freeze the Baseline Player.”

    Goal: keep the back player under pressure and make the net player guess.

    • Aim: Deep cross-court to the baseline player, then occasional change of direction to test the net player.
    • Pattern Pair: Cross-court rallies from Live Cross + intentional poach looks from Doubles Systems.
    • Team Cue: “Freeze the back player; net stays active but patient.”
  • Double Net / Aggressive Poachers → “Lob Light On.”

    Goal: punish over-aggression at the net with height and smart lobs, then own the middle once they back up.

    • Aim: High, heavy cross-courts plus planned lobs over the more aggressive net player.
    • Pattern Pair: Reset ideas from Special Situations (especially vs. pressure) + net movement from Transition & Net once they drop back.
    • Team Cue: “Height first, then take their net away when they retreat.”
  • Australian / I-Formation → “Hit Through the Middle, Then Adjust.”

    Goal: avoid overreacting to the stunt. Use big-margin targets until you read their pattern, then counter.

    • Aim: First plan: deep middle or heavy cross-court away from the poaching lane. Second plan: occasional hard body ball at the net player if they guess early.
    • Pattern Pair: Role clarity from Doubles Systems (“Server+net set the play”) + safe returns using Return+1 Schemes.
    • Team Cue: “Middle first; we beat the stunt with margin, not panic.”

Match Standard: By mid-set, every player can name the opponent archetype or formation and one counter-pattern they’re using against it.

Coach’s Cue: “Name it, then aim it.” Once you’ve named who you’re playing, the patterns on this page tell you where to aim and how to build pressure.

Match Metrics & Review in Action

On the Strategy page, you saw the core numbers we care about: first-serve percentage, return depth, and pattern success. This section shows how to test patterns from this page against those numbers and build a simple feedback loop after every match.

The goal is not to walk around with a spreadsheet in your head. The goal is to pick one serve pattern and one return pattern, track them in a simple way, and use the results to decide what to train next week.

Step 1 — Choose 1–2 Patterns to Test

Before a match or practice set, pick a small focus: one Serve+1 pattern and one Return+1 pattern you want to test.

  • Serve focus: “Body serve, +1 deep middle” from Serve+1 Trees.
  • Return focus: “Big-margin cross-court, recover middle” from Return+1 Schemes.
  • Optional: Add one neutral pattern test — e.g. “Middle Win” from Neutral Patterns.

Write these on your match card or in your notes: “Serve: body +1 middle. Return: cross, recover middle. Neutral: Middle Win.”

Step 2 — Track Simple Checkmarks, Not Every Point

You don’t need full stats. Use checkmarks at changeovers to capture trends:

  • After each of your serve games, ask: “Did I call and run my Serve+1 pattern at least once that game?” If yes, mark a ✅ next to Serve+1 on your card.
  • After each of their serve games, ask: “Did I call and run my Return+1 pattern at least once that game?” If yes, mark a ✅ next to Return+1.
  • During long rallies, note a ✅ when you remember to use your neutral focus (e.g., Middle Win or Height First).

You’re tracking behavior first (“Did I run the pattern?”), not just outcome. The metrics on the Strategy page (first-serve %, return depth, success rate) make more sense once you know whether you actually stuck to the plan.

Step 3 — 5-Minute Post-Match Review

After the match, use the Strategy page questions and add one line per pattern:

  1. First-serve percentage: Rough estimate — did you feel above or below the team standard? If below, note which Serve+1 pattern broke down first.
  2. Return depth: How often did your Return+1 land past the service line? Under 50%? That’s your first practice target.
  3. Pattern success: For each focus pattern, write: “Ran it about X times, won about Y of those points.”
  4. One adjustment: Choose one change for next time (earlier call, safer target, more height, etc.).

Keep this review to five minutes. The goal is a quick loop: pattern → match → note → next practice.

Step 4 — Turn Notes into Next-Week Reps

Bring your Match Metrics notes to the next practice and attach them to specific drills:

  • Low first-serve % with your Serve+1 pattern? Use serve boxes games from Drills and call the pattern out loud before every rep.
  • Shallow returns on your Return+1 pattern? Run return depth drills and only count reps that land past the service line.
  • Neutral pattern broke down under pressure? Add scoreboard pressure (30–30, deuce starts) while you run that pattern from the Pressure Packages section.

Team Standard: For every dual match, each player tracks one Serve+1 pattern, one Return+1 pattern, and writes one line of feedback to bring to the next practice.

Coach’s Cue: “If we can’t describe how a pattern performed, we can’t truly improve it.” Use this section and the Strategy metrics together so your patterns get sharper all season.

Tactical Checklist

On the Strategy Checklist you answered big-picture questions: Who am I as a player? What is my A-plan and backup plan? How do I want this match to feel? This Tactical Checklist is the point-by-point version of that work.

Use it between points. You don’t need to talk through every line; just hit these steps in your head (or quietly with your partner) so every point has a clear identity and a clear pattern.

  1. Confirm the plan tile.

    In one sentence, match the moment to your Strategy Checklist: “I’m in Serve-First with my A-plan,” or “We’re in Neutral-to-Offense against a counter-puncher.”

  2. Call one pattern out loud.

    Name a specific play from this page: “Body serve, +1 deep middle,” “Cross-court return, recover middle,” or “Live Cross until short, then approach.”

  3. Lock the first ball to a big-margin target.

    Serve/return or first rally ball goes to a safe zone: cross-court or deep middle in singles, body or middle in doubles. No hero lines on the first swing.

  4. Finish only when balanced and earned.

    Use your pattern to move them first, then finish when you’re inside the court and on balance. If you’re stretched or late, reset with height and depth instead of forcing a winner.

  5. Micro-review at the back fence.

    Win or lose, ask one quick question: “Did we run the pattern we called?” If not, the next point is a redo with the same pattern.

Turn this into a simple between-point checklist you can literally “tick” in your head:

  • ⬜ I matched the moment to my Strategy tile (plan).
  • ⬜ I called one clear pattern (play).
  • ⬜ My first ball went to a big-margin target.
  • ⬜ I only went for the finish when I was balanced.
  • ⬜ I checked whether we actually ran what we called.

Team Standard: In every dual match, aim to hit at least 3 of 5 Tactical Checklist boxes on 80% of points in your position.

Coach’s Cue: Strategy Checklist = “Who are we and what’s the plan?” Tactical Checklist = “What exact play are we calling on this point?” Use them together so your identity and your choices stay aligned all season.

Serve+1 Trees

Return+1 Schemes

Neutralize the serve with a big-margin return, then attack predictable replies. Step in on second serves whenever you can.

Target: Win about 45% of return games in practice sets.

Neutral Patterns

These patterns keep you safe while you wait for a good chance to attack.

  • Live Cross: Rally cross-court until you get short/middle/height; then attack.
  • Middle Win: Recover inside the hash; aim deep middle first.
  • Height First: Add 2–3 feet of net clearance when rushed or off balance.

Reinforces Strategy → Core Pillars.

Transition & Net

Use clear, repeatable patterns for getting forward and finishing points at the net.

  • Approach → First Volley: Approach with height and depth to the corner, then first volley deep middle.
  • Poach Trigger: Call the serve/return target; partner crosses on the predictable reply.
  • Reset & Close: If stretched, play high middle, then close diagonally to cut off the next ball.

Reinforces Strategy → Game Plans.

Doubles Systems

Give each team a simple system so everyone knows their role before the serve.

  • Australian / I-Formation: Hide weaknesses and pre-plan the poach direction.
  • Role Clarity: Server+net sets the play; partner calls lanes and body targets.
  • Middle First: Take away angles and force opponents to beat you through the middle first.

Reinforces Strategy → Archetypes & Core Pillars.

Pressure Packages

“Packages” are small sets of plays you call when you want to raise pressure without getting reckless.

  • Body First: Jam the hips; earn the next ball into open space.
  • Depth Lock: Hit three balls deep middle before trying an angle.
  • Height Lock: When nervous, add margin and avoid low-percentage down-the-line bail-outs.

Reinforces Strategy → Scoreboard Rules.

Special Situations

When things get chaotic, go back to the three anchors from your Strategy page: depth to reset, width to move them, and pace only when you’re balanced.

Use a small set of defaults so players aren’t guessing in tricky conditions or big moments.

  • Wind / Sun: Favor margin and body targets; use high, heavy resets to buy time.
  • Lefty / Unusual Spins: Aim middle first; make them hit away from their strength.
  • Big Points: Call your highest-confidence play — deep middle or body — then commit fully.

Reinforces Strategy → Environment & Scoreboard Rules.

If your first-serve percentage, return depth, or chosen pattern success rate starts to dip, simplify: call one safe pattern and run it for an entire game to reset your timing and confidence.

Coach’s Corner

Tactics

Focus Theme

Use the Compass — Tempo, Space, Identity, Pressure — to make quick, clear tactical decisions. Strategy sets the plan; Tactics is how you steer point by point when the match gets messy.

The Compass connects directly to your strategic identity: when in doubt, use Tempo, Space, Identity, and Pressure to steer back toward your A-plan patterns.

North — Tempo (How fast should this point feel?)

  • Slow it down: add height and spin, rally cross-court, and reset to deep middle when rushed.
  • Hold neutral tempo: steady rally speed, big margins, win “boring” points with shape.
  • Speed up on purpose: step inside the baseline on short or middle balls; attack with clear targets.
  • Coach question at changeovers: “Do we need to slow this match down or speed it up?”

East — Space (Where are we trying to play the point?)

  • Middle first: deep middle to take away angles and calm things down when you’re leaking errors.
  • Wide second: pull them off the court only once you’re balanced and inside the court.
  • Short by design: planned drop shots or short angles, not “oops” balls that sit up.
  • Coach cue: “Middle until we’ve earned space.” Pair with Core Pillars.

South — Identity (Whose patterns are we playing?)

  • Play your strengths: forehand-favored patterns for forehand players, net-first patterns for strong volleyers.
  • Avoid their comfort zone: don’t live in their favorite rally shape (for example, feeding a big forehand over and over).
  • Use this page’s Serve+1, Return+1, and Neutral patterns to define 2–3 “identity plays” per player.
  • Coach question: “Right now, are we in our pattern or theirs?”

West — Pressure (How are we applying or releasing stress?)

  • Apply pressure: depth to the baseline, body targets on serve/return, and heavy cross-court to the weaker wing.
  • Release pressure: high, heavy reset to deep middle, or a planned lob to buy space and time.
  • Scoreboard aware: at 30–30, deuce, and tie-break points, favor high-margin pressure plays over low-margin finishes.
  • Connect to Scoreboard Rules and this page’s Pressure Packages.

Compass Huddle Drill

In a timeout or changeover huddle, pick one Compass direction to adjust: Tempo, Space, Identity, or Pressure. Have the player say it back in a short cue (for example, “Slow tempo, middle first,” or “Use my forehand pattern more.”) Then track the next three games: did they actually change how fast they played, where they played, which patterns they chose, or how they applied pressure? Goal: player can name the Compass adjustment and give one example point where they used it.
