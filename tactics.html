Tactics

Strategy is your match-wide plan. Tactics are your point-by-point play calls. Everything on this page is a practical way to express the Four P’s—Percentages, Pressure, Patience, Position—from your Strategy page.

Before each point, use your Between-Point Routine (BPR) to choose one pattern for the next point: serve or return, singles or doubles, building, pressuring, or resetting. Say it, own it, and run it with commitment.

Treat this page like your personal Pattern Menu: Serve+1 and Return+1 trees, neutral rally patterns, transition plays, net-attack sequences, doubles systems, pressure packages, and special situations that plug directly into your Strategic Game Plans (Serve-First, Return-First, Neutral-to-Offense, Defense-to-Reset).

Coach’s Cue: “Plan → Pattern → Commit.” Strategy sets the plan. This page gives you the pattern. Your BPR helps you commit to the next point only—so you play with intention instead of drifting.

Pattern Index: Call the Play

On Strategy at a Glance Strategy is the map for how we want Jackman Solent to play. This Pattern Index is the playbook: the exact point-by-point patterns you can call before a serve, return, or rally.

How to use this page: pick one pattern, run it for 2–3 points, then reassess. Don’t bounce between ideas. Commitment beats variety.

Every pattern works in both singles and doubles. When a pattern leans strongly one way, you’ll see a [Singles Focus] or [Doubles Focus] tag.

  • Core Pillars (Four P’s & Four T’s) → High-Percentage Plays

    Turn Percentages, Pressure, Patience, and Position into repeatable wins. Start with Neutral Patterns and layer in Pressure Packages. In doubles, stack them with Doubles Systems while the Four T’s keep you connected.

  • Between-Point Routine (BPR) → Call the Pattern

    Use BPR’s Plan → Breathe → Commit to choose the pattern before the point starts. Pair one play from Serve+1 or Return+1 with full commitment.

  • Scoreboard Rules → Pressure Packages

    When the score tightens (30–30, deuce, tie-break), lean on big-margin, rehearsed patterns: Serve+1, Return+1, and stabilizing Pressure Packages.

  • Environment & Adjustments → Situational Plays

    Attach your wind, sun, or surface plan to Neutral Patterns and Special Situations. Example: windy day → “High, deep middle” + height first.

  • Strategic Game Plans → Point Trees

    Each Game Plan has a matching pattern family: Serve-First → Serve+1; Return-First → Return+1; Neutral-to-Offense & Defense-to-Reset → Neutral and Transition & Net.

  • Opponent Archetypes & Formations → Counter-Patterns

    Once you name the archetype or formation, choose patterns from Neutral, Pressure, and Doubles Systems that stress what they handle worst.

  • Match Metrics & Review → Test the Patterns

    Use your metrics (serve %, return depth, pattern success) to evaluate patterns from Serve+1, Return+1, and Neutral. Keep what holds up under pressure.

  • Strategy Checklist → Tactical Checklist

    Strategy answers “Who am I and what’s my plan?” The Tactical Checklist answers “What exact play am I calling next?” Use both so every point has identity and execution.

Top Strategy Principles in Action

On the Strategy page you saw the “Top 10 Strategy Principles.” This section turns each one into a default tactical behavior: clear targets, short cues, and direct links to the patterns below.

When a point or game slips away, find the principle you broke, then pick the matching play and commit to it for the next game.

  1. Own the Middle → “Recover Inside the Hash.”

    After every shot, recover so you could touch the center hash in one or two steps. In rallies, live in deep middle until you’ve clearly earned width.

    Pair with: Middle Win.

  2. Play High-Percentage Targets → “Big Zones, Big Net Clearance.”

    Choose cross-court or deep middle 80–90% of the time on neutral balls. Add 2–3 feet of net clearance by default, especially when under stress.

    Pair with: Height First.

  3. Move Them Before You Finish → “Two to Move, One to Hurt.”

    Commit to two strong rally balls that push them deep or wide before you look for the finish. Create space first, attack second.

    Pair with: cross-court patterns from Live Cross, then forward options in Transition & Net.

  4. Balance Before Power → “No Hero Swings Off-Balance.”

    If you’re drifting, stretched, or off-balance, your only job is to reset: high, deep, usually through the middle. Save power for when your base is strong.

    Pair with: Special Situations and Height First.

  5. Use Depth to Stabilize → “Deep Middle Reset.”

    When a rally feels chaotic, send a high, heavy ball deep middle and recover inside the hash. Make them hit from the back again.

    Pair with: Depth Lock.

  6. Know Your Patterns → “Name Your A-Play.”

    Have one go-to pattern on serve and one on return. Say it before big points: “Wide serve, +1 deep middle” or “Cross-court return, recover middle.”

    Pair with: Serve+1 Trees and Return+1 Schemes.

  7. Target Their Weakness → “Lean Without Becoming Predictable.”

    Send most neutral balls to their weaker side or weaker movement direction, but sprinkle in height or direction changes so they can’t camp.

    Pair with: Pressure Packages and Doubles Systems.

  8. Recover to the Right Spot → “Center Between Threats.”

    After you hit, recover to the midpoint between their two strongest replies. Usually that’s just inside the singles court, shaded slightly toward where you just hit.

    Pair with: recovery cues in Neutral Patterns.

  9. Play the Score → “Risk Matches the Moment.”

    On big points (30–30, deuce, tie-break), choose your highest-margin patterns: deep middle or body, then build pressure. Use creative looks when you’re ahead.

    Pair with: Serve+1, Return+1, and Pressure Packages.

  10. Make One More Ball → “Boring Wins Under Pressure.”

    When nerves spike, your job is to put one more high-margin ball in play. No line hunting, no last-second changes — just depth, shape, and recovery.

    Pair with: high-percentage patterns from Neutral Patterns and resets in Special Situations.

Coach’s Cue: When a point goes wrong, don’t guess. Ask “Which principle did we break?” Then choose the matching play from this list for the next game.

Core Pillars in Motion: Four P’s & Four T’s

The Four P’s (Percentages, Pressure, Patience, Position) and Four T’s (Turn, T, Talk, Touch) define how we want Jackman Solent to play. This section turns them into simple defaults you can use before each point.

When a match feels messy, don’t invent something new. Pick one P and, in doubles, recommit to the Four T’s between every point. Then choose a matching pattern from this page and run it for the next game.

Percentages → “Big Targets, Big Shape”

Percentages means choosing big zones, high net clearance, and safe directions. Under pressure, you win by picking the largest margin.

  • Singles: Default to cross-court or deep middle with 2–3 feet of net clearance on neutral balls.
  • Doubles: Default to deep middle or body on serve and return to shrink angles and reduce chaos.
  • Pattern Pair: Start with Neutral Patterns (“Live Cross,” “Middle Win,” “Height First”) plus the Tactical Checklist.

Pressure → “Stack Good Balls Before You Finish”

Pressure isn’t “swing harder.” It’s stacking quality balls that push an opponent into weaker positions until they crack.

  • Singles: Commit to two or three deep, heavy balls before looking for a winner.
  • Doubles: Use body serves, deep middle returns, and active net movement to crowd space and force rushed contact.
  • Pattern Pair: Call a Pressure Package (“Body First,” “Depth Lock,” “Height Lock”) when you’re ahead in the game.

Patience → “Win the Boring Point”

Patience means accepting the longer, smarter rally instead of chasing a low-percentage finish. Heavy, consistent balls force errors on their own.

  • Singles: On tough days, commit to “no hero down-the-line” unless you’re balanced and inside the court.
  • Doubles: Keep the ball cross-court and middle until you’ve earned a short ball or high volley.
  • Pattern Pair: Use Neutral Patterns and the “boring wins” mindset from Special Situations.

Position → “Own the Middle, Then Earn the Angle”

Position is about where you stand and where you recover. Our default: own the middle until you’ve clearly earned space.

  • Singles: Recover just inside the singles court, shaded slightly toward where you hit. Don’t camp in the alley unless the point demands it.
  • Doubles: Move as a unit — when one player shifts, the other mirrors to protect middle.
  • Pattern Pair: Combine recovery cues from Neutral Patterns with forward movement in Transition & Net.

[Doubles Focus] Four T’s → “Reset After Every Point”

The Four T’s keep your communication and connection rock-solid. Treat them as a non-negotiable reset between every doubles point.

  • Turn: First step goes toward your partner, not the fence.
  • T: Meet or glance at the “T” (center/serve line cross) to reset spacing.
  • Talk: One clear phrase: recognition, encouragement, or plan.
  • Touch: Racquets, fist bump, or shoulder tap — a small gesture that says, “We’re in this together.”

Doubles Standard: Aim for Four T’s on 90% of points.

Coach’s Cue: “Pick one P and play to it.” If you feel lost, choose Percentages, Pressure, Patience, or Position as your focus for the next two games and select matching patterns from this page.

BPR in Action: Call-the-Play Scripts

Your BPR gives you the steps: Plan → Breathe → Commit. This section turns that routine into short, repeatable scripts you can use to call patterns from this page.

Treat these like mini huddles. Before each point, plug in the score, your situation (serve / return, singles / doubles), and one pattern from this page that fits.

Step 1 — Plan: “What’s the play?”

In the first seconds after a point ends, quietly answer:

  • Scoreboard: Am I ahead, behind, or even?
  • Strengths: Which pattern fits my strengths?
  • Target: Where is the first ball going?

Then choose one pattern from: Serve+1 Trees, Return+1 Schemes, or Neutral Patterns.

Step 2 — Breathe: “Slow the movie down.”

Before you walk to the line or return position, take one full breath: in through your nose, out through your mouth. Use that exhale to release the last point.

  • Physical: Drop shoulders, soften jaw, loosen grip 10–20%.
  • Mental: “Next point only” or “Big margins here.”
  • Pattern: Picture the first ball’s height, shape, and target.

Step 3 — Commit: “Say it and own it.”

Commitment means you stop shopping for other ideas once you pick the play. Say a short script to yourself (or partner), then trust it fully.

Use these sample scripts as a guide:

  • [Singles Serve] “30–15. Body serve, +1 deep middle. ‘Body, middle, recover.’”
  • [Singles Return] “15–30. Cross return, recover middle. ‘Big margin cross, middle first.’”
  • [Doubles Serve Team] “Deuce. T serve, partner poach. ‘T serve, middle first volley.’”
  • [Doubles Return Team] “30–30. Deep middle return, partner active. ‘Middle return, you ready to move.’”

BPR Pattern Pairings

Make BPR automatic by pre-selecting a few pairings:

  • Serve Default: BPR + one Serve+1 Tree.
  • Return Default: BPR + one Return+1 Scheme.
  • Neutral Default: BPR + one Neutral Pattern.
  • Pressure Default: BPR + one Pressure Package.

Match Standard: Aim to run BPR on 80% of points.

Coach’s Cue: “Don’t just reset your mind — reset your pattern.” BPR is not just calming down; it’s choosing one clear play before every serve or return.

Scoreboard Rules in Action: Green, Yellow, Red

Your Scoreboard Rules show you how to adjust risk by score instead of emotion. Here we turn that idea into simple packages: Green (go), Yellow (steady), Red (lock it down).

Use them like traffic lights. Check the score, name the color, then choose one pattern from this page that matches that level of risk.

Green Light Points → “Earned Risk”

Green: score gives you cushion — 30–0, 40–15, 40–0 on your serve; 0–30 or 15–40 on theirs. This is where you test a new pattern or add controlled aggression inside your identity.

  • Singles: Trusted direction change (cross → line) or planned short angle after you’ve moved them.
  • Doubles: Planned poach or confident attack at the net player’s feet.
  • Pattern Pair: Green → more Transition & Net and favorite Serve+1 Trees.

Yellow Light Points → “Big Margins, Clear Patterns”

Yellow: neutral or early-game scores — 0–0, 15–15, 30–30, 15–0, 0–15. Run your A-plan patterns: big zones, high margins, no surprise changes unless clearly earned.

  • Singles: Cross or deep middle with 2–3 feet of net clearance; “Two to move, one to hurt.”
  • Doubles: Serve and return body or middle, then rally through middle until a clean short ball appears.
  • Pattern Pair: Yellow → your standard Neutral Patterns and usual Return+1 Scheme.

Red Light Points → “Lock the Doors”

Red: pressure moments — 30–40, break point, deuce, tie-break points, or serving to stay in the set. Here we don’t get creative — we get disciplined.

  • Singles: First ball goes deep middle or safe cross. No line hunting, no last-second changes.
  • Doubles: Serve body, return body or middle; make the net player earn any poach.
  • Pattern Pair: Red → your highest-confidence Serve+1, Return+1, plus a Pressure Package.

[Doubles Focus] Game-Score Packages

Let the game score decide how bold your pattern will be. Captains and partners can use these short calls:

  • Up 30–0 / 40–15: “Green — poach on anything short.” Pair with an aggressive Doubles System.
  • Even 15–15 / 30–30: “Yellow — body first, middle second.”
  • Down 30–40 / Break Point: “Red — deep middle, no hero ball.”

Scoreboard Scripts + BPR

Tie Scoreboard directly into your BPR:

  • Green Script: “Up 40–15 — Green. Test the wide serve pattern.”
  • Yellow Script: “30–30 — Yellow. Big margin cross, recover middle.”
  • Red Script: “Break point — Red. Body serve, +1 deep middle.”

Match Standard: On every big point, you can say your color (Green / Yellow / Red) and the pattern you’re running before the serve.

Coach’s Cue: “Let the score choose the risk.” When in doubt, go back to Yellow or Red and run your safest patterns until the match settles.

Environment & Adjustments in Action

Wind, sun, and surface can make good players look wild. Your job isn’t to complain — it’s to build patterns that work with the conditions.

Use a simple rule at changeovers: “Name the factor → Name the adjustment → Name the pattern.” Then choose a play from Neutral Patterns or Special Situations and run it for the next game.

Wind Plans → “High, Deep, and Smart Targets”

Wind doesn’t have to be chaos. Let it help you.

  • Into the wind: Add height and spin, aim deep middle, and expect one extra ball to come back.
  • With the wind: Aim lower over the net and more toward middle so the ball doesn’t sail. Shape first, pace second.
  • Cross-wind: Rally mostly cross-court into the wind so misses drift long on the safe side instead of wide.
  • Pattern Pair: Combine wind adjustments with Height First and windy defaults in Special Situations.

Sun & Visibility → “Protect the Serve, Protect the Overhead”

Sun mostly affects serves, overheads, and high balls. Protect those shots and steer rallies where you can see.

  • Serving into the sun: Use more body and T serves with shape. Make them look into the sun, not you.
  • Overheads in the sun: Call “lob-light” for a game: fewer offensive lobs, more deep cross-court resets.
  • Doubles tweak: If your net partner faces glare, call patterns that keep balls lower through middle.
  • Pattern Pair: Tie sun plans to Serve+1 Trees and “Wind / Sun” defaults in Special Situations.

Surface & Bounce → “Match Your Shape to the Court”

Different courts reward different ball shapes. Choose patterns the court rewards.

  • Fast / low-bounce: Aim deep through middle, use Serve+1 and Return+1 to take time away early.
  • Slow / high-bounce: Use heavy spin and height, trust longer rallies, and lean into Neutral Patterns and Depth Lock.
  • Uneven bounces: Default to “high, heavy middle” and move your feet early. Let the bad bounce beat the ball, not you.
  • Pattern Pair: On tricky surfaces, tie your plan to Transition & Net: approach on your best-bouncing ball, then close.

Environment Scripts + BPR

Tie Environment directly into BPR so players name the factor and the fix:

  • Wind Script: “Strong cross — Height First, high deep middle.”
  • Sun Script: “Serving into sun — body serves only, +1 deep middle.”
  • Surface Script: “Fast court — Serve+1 to body, first strike ready.”
  • [Doubles] Team Script: “Wind off the right — middle-heavy, poach only on short balls.”

Match Standard: At each changeover, players can name one environment factor, one adjustment, and one pattern they’re using because of it.

Coach’s Cue: “The conditions aren’t the excuse; they’re the game plan.”

Strategic Game Plans in Action

The four big Game Plans—Serve-First, Return-First, Neutral-to-Offense, Defense-to-Reset—tell you how this game should feel. This section turns each one into a pattern tree you can call.

At a changeover, pick one Game Plan and say: “For this game we are in <Plan> mode.” Then choose matching patterns from Serve+1, Return+1, Neutral, Transition & Net, and Pressure Packages.

Serve-First Plan → “Win the First Strike”

Call this when you trust your serve and first ball. Goal: hold serve efficiently and apply early pressure.

  • [Singles] “Body or wide serve; +1 deep middle. Attack open space only when balanced.”
  • [Doubles] “Body serve; net player active. +1 deep middle; poach when earned.”
  • Tree: Serve+1 Trees + scoreboard-aware Pressure Packages.

Return-First Plan → “Neutralize, Then Squeeze”

Call this when their serve is strong or streaky. Goal: get the serve back with margin and force extra balls.

  • [Singles] “Big-margin cross return; recover middle; play high heavy neutral.”
  • [Doubles] “Deep middle or body return; net player ready but patient. No first-ball hero shots.”
  • Tree: Return+1 Schemes + stabilizing Neutral Patterns.

Neutral-to-Offense Plan → “Two to Move, One to Hurt”

Call this when rallies are even and you need to turn neutral into pressure without rushing.

  • [Singles] “Two heavy cross-courts to move them; then attack the short ball.”
  • [Doubles] “Cross-court and middle first; net closes only after we push them back.”
  • Tree: Neutral → then forward through Transition & Net.

Defense-to-Reset Plan → “Survive, Reset, Rebuild”

Call this when they’re dictating, you’re stretched, or the score is tight. Goal: reset the point, not flip it in one swing.

  • [Singles] “When stretched: high heavy deep middle; recover inside the hash.”
  • [Doubles] “Lob-light mode: high deep middle resets or safe lobs; protect middle together.”
  • Tree: Resets from Special Situations + safety-first Depth Lock and Height First.

Match Standard: At every changeover, players can name which Game Plan they’re in and one pattern they’re using.

Coach’s Cue: “Plan the game, then run the tree.”

Opponent Archetypes & Formations in Action

Once you name the opponent type or doubles formation, the point becomes less random. This section turns those labels into counter-patterns you can call.

Use: Name → Aim → Pattern. Name the archetype or formation, choose a primary aim (middle, body, height, or width), then pick a pattern from this page that stresses their weakness.

[Singles Focus] Common Archetypes → Counter-Patterns

Default responses you can refine with your own strengths.

  • Big Hitter → “Shrink the Court, Raise the Net.”

    Goal: take away their targets. Live in deep middle with big margins and make them earn angles.

    • Aim: Deep middle and body, especially on big points.
    • Pattern Pair: Neutral Patterns (“Middle Win,” “Height First”) + Depth Lock.
    • Coach Cue: “Make them beat you through the middle, not with highlights.”
  • Counter-Puncher / Defender → “Own the Middle, Finish at the Net.”

    Goal: avoid endless rallies. Use depth to push them back, then finish forward instead of trying to blast through from the baseline.

    • Aim: Deep cross and middle until you earn a short ball, then approach.
    • Pattern Pair: Cross-courts from Live Cross → forward through Transition & Net.
    • Coach Cue: “Don’t out-grind the grinder — take the net.”
  • Pusher / Human Backboard → “Boring Wins with Planned Finishes.”

    Goal: stay patient but not passive. You win by repeating heavy, safe balls and having a planned finish.

    • Aim: High, heavy cross-courts, then one committed finish (short angle, approach, or net).
    • Pattern Pair: “Win the boring ball” from Neutral Patterns + a chosen finish from Transition & Net.
    • Coach Cue: “Heavy, patient, then one committed finish — not ten ideas.”
  • All-Courter / Problem Solver → “Anchor to Your Identity.”

    Goal: avoid getting dragged into their variety show. Keep returning to your A-patterns and make them prove they can beat that identity.

    • Aim: Play into the patterns you trained most (forehand-favored, net-first, etc.).
    • Pattern Pair: Use your chosen Serve+1 and Return+1 “A-plays,” plus your favorite Neutral Pattern.
    • Coach Cue: “Win with your game first; adjust only if they truly solve it.”

[Doubles Focus] Common Formations → Team Responses

In doubles, the formation tells you where the space is. Use it to choose simple team patterns instead of guessing.

  • Both Back → “Take the Net and Own the Middle.”

    Goal: punish passive court position. Close the net together and force low-percentage passes.

    • Aim: Approach deep middle, first volley deep middle, then close.
    • Pattern Pair: Transition & Net (“Approach → First Volley → Close”) + middle-first language from Doubles Systems.
    • Team Cue: “Both up, protect middle, live with the occasional pass.”
  • One Up / One Back → “Freeze the Baseline Player.”

    Goal: keep the back player under pressure and make the net player guess.

    • Aim: Deep cross-court to the baseline player, then occasional direction change to test the net player.
    • Pattern Pair: Cross-courts from Live Cross + intentional poach looks from Doubles Systems.
    • Team Cue: “Freeze the back player; net stays active but patient.”
  • Double Net / Aggressive Poachers → “Lob Light On.”

    Goal: punish over-aggression at net with height and smart lobs, then own the middle once they back up.

    • Aim: High, heavy cross-courts plus planned lobs over the more aggressive net player.
    • Pattern Pair: Reset ideas from Special Situations + net movement from Transition & Net once they drop back.
    • Team Cue: “Height first; when they retreat, we take the net.”
  • Australian / I-Formation → “Hit Through the Middle, Then Adjust.”

    Goal: avoid overreacting to the stunt. Use big-margin targets until you read their pattern, then counter.

    • Aim: First plan: deep middle or heavy cross away from the poach lane. Second plan: occasional hard body ball at the net player if they guess early.
    • Pattern Pair: Role clarity from Doubles Systems + safe returns using Return+1 Schemes.
    • Team Cue: “Middle first; we beat the stunt with margin, not panic.”

Match Standard: By mid-set, every player can name the opponent archetype or formation and one counter-pattern they’re using.

Coach’s Cue: “Name it, then aim it.”

Match Metrics & Review in Action

Metrics matter only if they connect to patterns. This section shows you how to test patterns from this page against your key numbers: first-serve %, return depth, pattern success.

The goal is not a spreadsheet in your head. The goal is: one Serve+1 pattern, one Return+1 pattern, simple marks, and a quick feedback loop into practice.

Step 1 — Choose 1–2 Patterns to Test

Before a match or practice set, choose: one Serve+1 and one Return+1 to evaluate.

  • Serve: “Body serve, +1 deep middle” from Serve+1 Trees.
  • Return: “Big-margin cross, recover middle” from Return+1 Schemes.
  • Optional neutral test — e.g., “Middle Win” from Neutral Patterns.

Step 2 — Track Checkmarks, Not Every Point

You don’t need full stats. Use checkmarks at changeovers:

  • After your serve games: “Did I call and run my Serve+1 at least once?” If yes, add a ✔️ next to Serve+1.
  • After their serve games: “Did I call and run my Return+1 at least once?” ✔️ next to Return+1 if yes.
  • In long rallies, add ✔️ every time you remember your neutral focus (Middle Win, Height First, etc.).

Track behavior first (“Did I run the pattern?”), then results.

Step 3 — 5-Minute Post-Match Review

After the match, use the Strategy questions and add one line per pattern:

  1. First-serve %: above or below the standard? Where did Serve+1 break down?
  2. Return depth: how often did Return+1 land past the service line?
  3. Pattern success: “Ran it about X times, won about Y of those points.”
  4. One adjustment for next time (earlier call, safer target, more height, etc.).

Step 4 — Turn Notes into Reps

Bring your notes into practice and attach them to specific drills:

  • Low first-serve % on Serve+1? Use serve-box games from Drills and call the pattern out loud before each rep.
  • Shallow returns on Return+1? Run return-depth drills and only count balls past the service line.
  • Neutral pattern broke under pressure? Add scoreboard pressure (30–30, deuce starts) while running that pattern with Pressure Packages.

Team Standard: For every dual match, each player tracks one Serve+1, one Return+1, and writes one line of feedback for next practice.

Coach’s Cue: “If we can’t describe how a pattern performed, we can’t truly improve it.”

Tactical Checklist

The Strategy Checklist answers big-picture questions: Who am I? What’s my A-plan and backup? How do I want this match to feel? The Tactical Checklist is the point-by-point version.

Use it between points. You don’t have to say every line out loud — just run these steps quickly so every point has a clear identity and a specific pattern.

  1. Confirm the plan tile.

    “I’m in Serve-First,” or “We’re in Neutral-to-Offense vs. a counter-puncher.”

  2. Call one pattern out loud.

    “Body serve, +1 deep middle,” “Cross return, recover middle,” or “Live Cross until short, then approach.”

  3. Lock the first ball to a big-margin target.

    Serve/return or first rally ball goes to a safe zone: cross or deep middle in singles; body or middle in doubles.

  4. Finish only when balanced and earned.

    Move them first. Finish when you’re inside the court and on balance. If you’re stretched or late, reset with height and depth.

  5. Micro-review at the back fence.

    “Did we run the pattern we called?” If not, redo the same pattern on the next point.

Turn it into a mental checklist:

  • ⬜ I matched the moment to my Strategy tile (plan).
  • ⬜ I called one clear pattern (play).
  • ⬜ My first ball went to a big-margin target.
  • ⬜ I only finished when I was balanced.
  • ⬜ I checked whether we ran what we called.

Team Standard: Aim to hit at least 3 of 5 boxes on 80% of points.

Coach’s Cue: Strategy = “Who are we and what’s the plan?” Tactics = “What exact play are we calling on this point?”

Serve+1 Trees

Serve+1 is your default attacking system on serve. Call a primary serve target (T, body, or wide), then pair it with a high-margin +1 to deep middle or body. The goal is simple: start safe, land the first ball, and earn the right to attack space.

This works because the first two balls shape the point. When you commit to a clear script — “Body serve → +1 deep middle” — your feet, spacing, and decisions stay calm. You’re not improvising; you’re running a tree.

  • [Singles Default] Body or T serve → +1 deep middle. Change direction or attack wide only if the +1 creates a true attack ball (short, high, or slow and you’re balanced inside the court).
  • [Doubles Default] Body serve → partner active over middle → +1 deep middle. Poach or attack space only when the return floats, sits up, or comes through the middle without pace.
  • [Big-Point Version] On red points (break points, serving to stay in the set), eliminate choices: body or T serve → +1 deep middle → no last-second changes.

Goal: Win 65–70% of Serve+1 exchanges by committing to the default before you add risk.

Reinforces Strategy → Scoreboard Rules & Core Pillars.

Return+1 Schemes

Return+1 is your default system on their serve. First job: neutralize the serve with margin. Second job: use your +1 ball to reclaim court position and shift the rally in your favor.

Think of it as “get it back with margin → stabilize → then squeeze”. You are not hunting winners off the return. You are forcing them to hit one extra uncomfortable ball before you add pressure.

  • [Singles Default] Big-margin cross return → recover middle → +1 deep cross by default. Use deep middle only when rushed, stretched, or late. Do not change direction until you’ve earned balance inside the court.
  • [Doubles Default] Return deep middle or body → +1 back deep to the same player. Net partner owns the middle lane first — patient hands, no forced poach. Make them volley up or hit from behind the baseline.
  • [Big-Point Version] On red points (break points, game points against you), remove choices: high-margin return to middle or body → recover inside the hash → +1 deep middle. No line hunting. No last-second changes.

Target: In practice sets, aim to win about 45% of return games and land your Return+1 past the service line on 60%+ of points where you call it.

Reinforces Strategy → Game Plans & Scoreboard Rules.

Neutral Patterns

Neutral patterns are your default rally behaviors once the serve and return phase are over. When you are not clearly attacking or defending, these patterns keep you safe, stable, and in position while you wait for an Attack Ball.

Think: Serve+1 / Return+1 start the point, Neutral Patterns run the middle, then you upgrade into Pressure Packages or Transition & Net only when the ball earns it.

  • Live Cross: Rally cross-court until you earn something short, high, or clearly neutralized. You’re hitting over the lowest part of the net and into the longest part of the court. Default when the rally is even — especially right after a solid Serve+1 or Return+1.
  • Middle Win: Recover inside the hash and send your rally ball deep through the middle. This shrinks angles, forces predictable replies, and keeps you balanced. Default when you feel rushed, late, or facing a bigger hitter.
  • Height First: When rushed, stretched, or off balance, add 2–3 feet of net clearance. Height is your emergency brake that turns a losing ball back into neutral. Default reset when Serve+1 or Return+1 didn’t land cleanly but you can still recover.

Attack Ball Checklist

These are non-negotiable triggers. If none of them are present, you stay neutral. When one appears, you have the green light to upgrade Neutral → Pressure or Neutral → Transition & Net.

  • Short Ball: Lands inside the service line. → Upgrade to Approach → First Volley.
  • Middle Ball (with advantage): Sits central and gives you time, height, or balance. → Upgrade to Body First or continue Middle Win until the advantage grows.
  • High Ball: Shoulder-high or above, rising or slow. → Upgrade through Height First → Live Cross, then finish forward.
  • Weak Contact: Floater, slice under pressure, or off-balance reply. → Upgrade to Depth Lock or Approach & Finish.
  • Slow / Sitting Ball: Ball with time that lets you step inside. → Upgrade to Live Cross → Attack Open Space.
  • Opponent Late or Off-Balance: They’re stretched wide or recovering. → Upgrade to Transition & Net or a Pressure Package.

Coach’s Cue: “Neutral is your base. No trigger, no upgrade.”

Pressure Packages

Pressure Packages are small, named overlays you layer on top of your Serve+1, Return+1, and Neutral Patterns to raise pressure without chasing low-percentage winners. They tighten targets, add depth or height, and force opponents to solve harder problems.

Think: call a Tree (Serve+1 / Return+1) → run Neutral → switch on a Package when the score, opponent, or conditions demand more discipline — not more speed.

  • Body First: Attach this to Serve+1 or Return+1 to jam the hips and steal time. First ball goes body or deep middle; second ball attacks the space you’ve opened. Script: “Body first, then space.” Use when you’re ahead, on Green/Yellow points, or against big swingers who hate being crowded.
  • Depth Lock: Commit to three straight balls deep through the middle before changing direction or pace. No lines. No last-second changes. Script: “Three deep middle, then earn the change.” Use when rallies feel rushed, emotional, or the opponent is heating up.
  • Height Lock: Add 2–3 feet of net clearance on purpose for a full game, tie-break, or service rotation. Height resets timing, calms your swing, and shuts down panic decisions. Script: “Height first, patterns second.” Use on Red points, in wind, or when nerves start pulling you off your patterns.

Treat Pressure Packages as discipline switches. Once you call one, you stay inside it until the situation changes. The goal isn’t instant winners — it’s forcing errors, short balls, and predictable replies.

Special Situations

When things get chaotic, return to three anchors: depth to reset, width to move them, pace only when you’re balanced.

Use these defaults so you’re never guessing in weird conditions, unusual matchups, or high-pressure moments.

  • Wind / Sun: Favor height, margin, and body targets. Lift high, heavy resets and let the elements make the rally hard for them, not you.
  • Lefty / Unusual Spins: Aim middle first to remove angles. Force them away from their strength and simplify your reads.
  • Big Points: Call your highest-confidence play — deep middle or body — then commit fully. Big points reward discipline, not hero shots.

Coach’s Corner

Tactics

Tactics turn your strategy identity into point-by-point execution. This page is your menu of executable plays: Serve+1, Return+1, neutral defaults, transition patterns, pressure packages, doubles systems, and special situations. This section explains why these patterns matter, what they look like when done well, and what breaks when pressure spikes.

Focus Theme

“Choose one pattern and run it for the full point.” The best tacticians don’t react — they decide early. Your BPR gives you the plan; your job is to attach that plan to a clear pattern and commit until the rally is over. Tactics succeed when your first ball matches your intention.

What to Reinforce

  • First-Ball Identity: The point is often decided by your intention on the first shot — not the finish.
  • High-Margin Defaults: Cross-court, deep middle, and +2–3 ft net clearance stabilize your game under pressure.
  • Linked Decisions: A good Serve+1 or Return+1 pattern naturally predicts your next two shots. Let the tree guide you.

Common Red Flags

  • Pattern Switching Mid-Point: Changing your mind while the ball travels leads to rushed feet and late contact.
  • Finishing Too Early: Trying to end the point before creating space — especially from behind the baseline.
  • Unclear Recovery: If you don’t know where to recover, you’ll leave angles open and get stretched wide.

Key Cues & Corrections

  • “Plan the first ball.” Say your target (serve, return, or rally cue) during your BPR.
  • “Big margins before big decisions.” Lift the ball first — attack later.
  • “Recover inside the hash.” Regain position immediately.
  • “Two to move, one to hurt.” Create space with two rally balls before attacking it.

Observation Drill (Captains & Coaches)

Score a live game using this simple metric:

Target: 8/10 points with a clearly identifiable pattern (Serve+1, Return+1, Neutral, Pressure, or Transition).

If the pattern isn’t obvious from the fence, the player didn’t commit early enough. Reset with BPR → choose → run.

Captain’s Checklist

Tactics

Use this as your on-court script. Your job is to keep each court running the patterns we’ve chosen — especially when matches get loud.

Before Match

  • Confirm each court’s Game Plan (Serve-First, Return-First, etc.).
  • Have players name one Serve+1 and one Return+1 pattern.
  • Set one cue per court: “Middle first,” “High margins,” or “Use my forehand pattern.”
  • For doubles: assign poach caller, middle-owner, and target caller.

During Games

  • Walk-by question #1: “What pattern are you running?”
  • Use the Compass (Tempo, Space, Identity, Pressure) — call the one that needs to change.
  • Watch for drift: hero lines, rushed winners, low-margin bailouts.
  • Reset with: “Slow tempo,” “Heavy cross,” “Middle first,” or “Play your pattern.”

Tight Points

  • Color call: “Red — body serve, +1 deep middle.”
  • Steer toward Pressure Packages (Body First, Depth Lock, Height Lock).
  • One breath, one cue: “Big margins,” “No hero ball,” or “Deep middle, recover inside the hash.”

Between Sets

  • Ask each player: “Which pattern is working? Which needs a tweak?”
  • Match the answer to a Compass word: Tempo, Space, Identity, or Pressure.
  • Re-anchor identity: “Win with your pattern first; adjust only if they truly solve it.”

Post-Match

  • Collect Serve+1 and Return+1 feedback cards.
  • Have them share one point where they executed the pattern cleanly.
  • Finish with one Compass adjustment for next practice.

Captain’s Cue: “Strategy gives the plan. Tactics give the play. Keep each court running the pattern we chose.”
