Forehand

At Poolesville, your forehand is a field-position weapon, not just a “big shot.” We build it around one repeatable pattern: unit turn → space → contact in front → tall finish.

Depth comes first, shape second, pace third. When your legs load the ground and your posture stays tall, the racquet can stay smooth and relaxed — the ball does the work for you.

Coach’s Cue: “Turn • Space • Front • Up.” Whisper it to yourself as the ball crosses the net until it feels automatic.

Forehand Fundamentals

Our goal is a forehand we can trust under varsity pressure — tiebreakers, break points, big games. Build the base first; the extra pace shows up once the pattern is solid.

  • Grip & Feel: Semi-western or eastern is fine. Keep the wrist soft, the hand relaxed, and the string-bed square at contact so the ball “sits” on the strings for a moment.
  • Unit Turn: Turn your shoulders as you split; racquet up and elbow relaxed. Show your back or shoulder logo to the target before the bounce.
  • Spacing: Create room with the outside foot. Keep a comfortable hip-to-hand distance so the swing path can stay smooth without jamming your body.
  • Contact in Front: Strike slightly ahead of the lead hip. Keep your head quiet at contact and let the ball “meet you in front,” not on top of you.
  • Finish Tall: Chest to target, palm-to-the-sky feeling, landing balanced on a strong base — not spinning off or drifting backward.

Consistency creates confidence; posture plus legs create pace. The racquet just delivers what your body builds.

Team Standard: 8/10 cross-court rally balls landing deep (past the service line) with safe height over the net strap.

Shapes & Targets

Every forehand needs a plan: shape + target. Decide it before the bounce. Margin first, scoreboard damage second.

  • Cross-Court (Default): Highest part of the net, longest piece of court. Use height, heavy spin, and deep targets as your baseline pattern.
  • Inside-Out: Step around from the backhand corner and send a big ball to their backhand. Recover through the middle so you’re not stuck in the alley.
  • Down-the-Line (Earned): Take this when you’re early, balanced, and seeing the ball well. Finish your weight into the court and be ready for the next ball back cross-court.
  • Body Middle: When you’re rushed, aim big middle to jam their strike zone, slow the point down, and buy time to reset your feet.

Goal: 6/10 balls landing inside a cross-court cone (service line to corner) and bouncing above hip height on your partner.

Footwork Patterns

Your feet write the story of the forehand. Match your move to the ball instead of using one swing for everything.

  • Step-Through: On short or mid-court balls, drive through contact and carry your momentum forward. Finish inside the baseline and look to finish the point.
  • Load-Hop: On higher pace, load on the outside leg, rotate through, then hop into a strong, balanced landing so you’re ready for the next ball.
  • Open Stance: On deep or wide balls, turn early, load on the outside leg, rotate through the shot, and finish tall without getting pulled off the court.
  • Emergency Block: When you’re late or stretched, shorten the swing, aim big middle, and use a compact block to buy yourself time to recover.

Your first step decides your shape. If the feet are late, the swing has to work too hard.

Pressure Plan

Tight score — 30-all, break point, tiebreak — is where our forehand identity matters most. Breathe, widen your targets, and trust your cross-court shape.

  • Default to high, heavy cross-court depth. Take the line only when you’re early, balanced, and the pattern clearly opens it.
  • Use body-middle to jam pace when you feel rushed. Make them swing under pressure instead of you.
  • Call your shape before the bounce: “XC high,” “IO heavy,” or “Middle jam” so the racquet is never guessing.

Reset Cue: “Turn • Space • Front • Up.” One deep breath, then commit to the pattern.

Forehand +1 Connection

The forehand rarely wins the point alone. Decide your next ball before you swing so your feet and eyes are already moving to the next job.

  • XC Heavy → Pull them wide cross-court, then attack the open court into the backhand corner or come forward behind it.
  • IO Big → Step through the inside-out forehand and look for the short ball. Be ready to finish to the open court or follow in if the ball sits up.
  • Middle Jam → Use the jam ball to rush their timing, then move the next forehand to space — deep cross-court or into the open lane.

Drills & Practice

  • XC Cone Ladder: 20-ball sets to a cross-court cone. Score only balls that land past the service line with safe height. Target: 16/20 or better before you raise the pace.
  • IO Step-Around: Coach or partner feeds to your backhand corner. Step around and drive inside-out to deep deuce. Goal: 8/10 balls deep with balanced recoveries back to neutral.
  • Earn the Line: Rally cross-court. When coach calls “Early!” take the line aggressively and then recover middle. Track your make-rate under that cue.

Common Errors & Quick Fixes

  • Late Contact: Balls flying long or pulled wide. Cue “Front!” earlier, shorten the backswing, and start the unit turn as the ball crosses the net.
  • Shanks: Crowding the ball or guessing on spacing. Add an outside adjustment step before you swing and keep your hitting arm relaxed.
  • Low Net Clips: Swing path too flat or aiming too low. Raise your finish, choose a higher net window, and feel the ball travel up and out.
  • Off-Balance Finish: Over-rotating or falling off the shot. Widen your base, stick the landing tall, and hold your finish for a count of one.

Self-check: Film 8–10 forehands from the side. Grade: (1) unit turn started before the bounce, (2) contact clearly in front of the lead hip, (3) finish tall with clean balance.

Forehand Checklist

  • ⬜ Early unit turn as the ball crosses the net
  • ⬜ Comfortable spacing with an outside-foot adjust step
  • ⬜ Contact clearly in front of the lead hip
  • ⬜ Tall, balanced finish with safe height over the net

Coach’s Corner

Forehand

Focus Theme

A depth-first forehand that wins field position under pressure. We care about a repeatable pattern — unit turn → spacing → contact in front → tall finish — that sends a heavy ball to big cross-court lanes before we hunt winners.

What to Reinforce

  • Turn early: shoulders and hips coil as the ball crosses the net so the swing never feels rushed.
  • Space the ball: create room with the outside foot; no crowding the hip or hand.
  • Contact in front + tall finish: strike slightly ahead of the lead hip, then finish high with chest to target and a quiet head.
  • Cross-court default: high, heavy XC as the baseline; line and inside-out are earned when early and balanced.

Common Red Flags

  • Late unit turn — racquet still facing the net as the ball bounces.
  • Crowded spacing — feet too close to the ball; contact jammed near the body.
  • Flat, low swing — balls clipping the tape instead of clearing the strap by a few feet.
  • Falling off the shot — over-rotation, landing narrow, or drifting backward.

Key Cues & Corrections

  • “Turn early, then space.”
  • “Front and up.” (Contact in front, finish high.)
  • “Big cross first.” (XC with height is the default.)
  • “Wide base, quiet head.”
  • If late or cramped, shrink the backswing and cue “outside step first” for 10 balls, all aimed cross-court with 3–4 feet of net clearance.

Observation Drill

Run a cross-court rally set of 20 forehands from coach feed or cooperative rally. Film from the side and track three things: (1) unit turn started before the bounce, (2) contact clearly in front of the lead hip, (3) finish tall with ball clearing the net strap by at least two ball heights. Target: 15 / 20 balls meeting all three checks, with at least 12 landing past the service line.

Captain’s Checklist

Forehand

Core Principles (Short Version)

Early turn, big space, contact in front, tall finish. Cross-court with shape is the default. Middle-jam when rushed; down-the-line only when early and balanced.

  • Turn as the ball crosses the net.
  • Outside step to create space.
  • Hit in front of the lead hip.
  • Finish high; chest to target.
  • XC heavy first — line is earned.

Fence Cues You Can Call

  • “Turn early.” (Shoulders, then racquet.)
  • “Space, then swing.” (Outside step, no crowding.)
  • “Big cross first.” (High, heavy XC as default.)
  • “Front and up.” (Contact in front, finish high.)
  • “Wide base, quiet head.” (Balance under pressure.)

Quick Checks Between Points

  • Is she turning as the ball crosses the net, or reacting late?
  • Is she creating space with the outside foot, or getting jammed?
  • Are misses into the tape (needs more shape) or wide/long (needs bigger targets)?
  • Is she finishing tall, or falling off the shot?
  • Is she playing mostly XC heavy, or forcing early line balls?

End-of-Set Snapshot

  • Ask: “If you hit 10 forehands that set, how many were deep cross-court?”
  • Ask: “When you missed, were you late and jammed, or early and over-swinging?”
  • Lock one focus for next set: turn earlier, create more space, or raise net clearance.
