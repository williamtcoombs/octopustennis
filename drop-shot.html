Change pace, change space. A trusted drop shot turns defense into doubt and drags opponents into your court.

Drop Shot Fundamentals

  • Same Setup: Prep like your drive — shoulder turn, racquet takeback — then soften late.
  • Soft Hands: Loosen the grip at contact so the strings cradle the ball; let the ball leave quietly.
  • Shape & Height: Slightly open face; brush up and short to clear the net with spin and kill the bounce.

The best drops look like drives until the last instant.

Goal: 7/10 drop shots landing within 6–8 feet of the net with the second bounce near or before the service line.

When to Use It

  • Opponent is deep behind the baseline or leaning back.
  • After heavy cross-court patterns that push them wide.
  • On slower, shorter balls you can take early from inside the court.
  • As a tempo change to break a hitter’s rhythm.

Cue: Think “short to open space,” not “perfect winner.” Make them run forward.

Technique Cues

  • Contact in front with a relaxed wrist; finish compact — no big follow-through.
  • Knife a touch of underspin (control) or cushion with a topspin fade (disguise) — choose the one you repeat.
  • Second bounce target: near or before the service line.
  • Recover forward: cut off the reply or be ready for the lob.

If you feel tense, exhale on contact and hold the finish for a one-count.

Patterns & Decisions

  • Heavy Cross → Drop Short: After moving them back and wide, drop into the open court.
  • Drop → Lob: If they sprint forward aggressively, lift the next ball over the backhand shoulder.
  • Drop → Pass: If they reach and float it, pass to the open lane. Racquet ready.
  • Inside-Court Read: Any ball inside the baseline and sitting high is a green light to show drop or drive.

Two-ball plans create confidence and clarity under pressure.

Drills

  • Cone Kiss: Place a cone 3–4 feet past the net; land the ball just beyond it ×10.
  • Disguise Series: Coach calls “drive” or “drop” late — same prep, different finish.
  • Drop–Lob Combo: Alternate soft drop with controlled lob to train read and response.
  • Approach Drop: Take early from inside the court; follow forward and split.

Weekly Challenge: Win 60% of points in practice where you attempt at least one drop shot by pairing it with a lob or pass on the next ball.

Common Errors

  • Telegraphing early with a big grip change or open face.
  • Hitting too hard — high, sit-up bounces that get punished.
  • Trying the drop from too deep behind the baseline.

Fix: Film 10 attempts. Grade: disguise (same prep), contact softness, second-bounce spot, recovery path.

Drop Shot Checklist

  • ⬜ Same prep as drive (disguise)
  • ⬜ Relaxed wrist + minimal follow-through
  • ⬜ Second bounce near service line
  • ⬜ Ready for lob or pass on next ball

Coach’s Corner

Drop Shot

Focus Theme

The drop shot is a control weapon, not a trick. The standard is: soft hands, disguise from drive prep, ball contact in front, and second bounce before the service line.

What to Reinforce

  • Disguise — same shoulder turn and preparation as a drive.
  • Quiet hands — absorb pace with a soft, upward finish.
  • Targets — second bounce well inside the service line.

Common Red Flags

  • Early “tell” — open racquet face too soon.
  • Contact too far back — reaching or flicking.
  • Drop shot landing too deep — opponent arrives comfortably.

Key Cues & Corrections

  • “Same prep.” (Hide it until the last moment.)
  • “Soft hands.” (Absorb pace; no punch.)
  • “Brush up.” (Shape the ball upward, not flat.)
  • “Second bounce early.” (Commit to depth.)

Observation Drill

Run a 20-ball drop-shot set from both deuce and ad. Track three things: disguise (prep), contact point (in front), and second-bounce depth. Targets: 14+/20 with clean disguise, 16+/20 with contact in front, and 12+/20 where the second bounce lands inside the service box hash mark. Choose one cue to emphasize in the next block.

Captain’s Checklist

Drop Shot

Core Principles (Short Version)

  • Disguise from full drive preparation.
  • Soft hands — absorb, don’t punch.
  • Second bounce inside the service line.
  • Use it only when the opponent is behind the baseline or off balance.

Fence Cues You Can Call

  • “Same prep.” (Don’t show it early.)
  • “Soft hands.” (Quiet the swing.)
  • “Second bounce early.” (Commit to depth.)
  • “Choose the right moment.” (Don’t gift momentum.)

Quick Checks Between Points

  • Is she disguising it, or opening the racquet too soon?
  • Is the contact point in front or drifting back?
  • Is she choosing the right situations (opponent deep/off balance)?
  • Is the second bounce landing well inside the service line?

End-of-Set Snapshot

  • Ask: “How many out of 10 had true disguise?”
  • Ask: “Where was your second bounce landing?”
  • Pick one focus for next set: prep disguise, soft hands, or depth.

Captain’s Cue: “Same prep, soft hands, early second bounce.” Keep it short, calm, and steady — your voice anchors the moment.
